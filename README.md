# study-of-groundhog-poop

A field-oriented, educational study of groundhog scat (droppings, feces) designed for naturalists, biologists, students, and curious citizens who want rigorous data with a side of humor. This repository collects observations, notes, protocols, and imagery to support safe, reproducible study of Marmota monax fecal evidence in the field.

---

## Overview

This project documents the morphology, composition, and ecological signals contained in groundhog droppings. Groundhog scat is an underutilized, non-invasive data source for:

- diet inference,
- habitat use,
- seasonal behavior,
- parasite and pathogen surveillance (with proper laboratory protocols),
- citizen science education.

Contents of this repo: field datasheets, photo catalog, identification notes, sample handling guidelines, and suggested analyses.

---

## Why it matters

Groundhogs are common mammalian herbivores with strong effects on vegetation structure and local soil processes. Studying their scat provides:

- Low-cost, non-invasive insight into diet and foraging ecology.
- Indicators of habitat quality and plant community composition.
- Baseline material for detecting shifts in diet due to land use or climate change.
- Opportunities for community engagement and ecological education.

Scat studies complement trapping and telemetry studies with minimal disturbance to animals.

## Morphology notes

Groundhog feces typically present with the following diagnostic descriptors:

- Shape and form: cylindrical to slightly tapered pellets or short tubular segments; occasionally clumped if recently deposited.
- Size: individual pellets ~8–15 mm in diameter, length variable; clumps up to several cm.
- Color: fresh droppings are dark green to brownish; aged droppings fade to tan/gray.
- Texture: fibrous, with visible plant material (stems, leaf fragments, grass); may be friable when dry.
- Odor: characteristic herbivore scent; less pungent than carnivore scat.
- Contents: undigested plant fibers, seeds, occasional bark or root fragments; parasite eggs may be present (requires lab testing).

Differentiation tips:
- Rabbit droppings are typically uniformly round pellets (~10–20 mm) and often in neat piles.
- Deer droppings are smaller, oblong pellets in larger quantities.
- Groundhog scat is coarser and often shows larger plant fragments than rabbit pellets.

Measure and record: pellet count per deposit, pellet dimensions (length × diameter), color (fresh/aged), moisture, and any visible contents.

## Habitat / Diet clues

Use scat observations to infer habitat use and diet composition:

- Visible plant fragments: broadleaf stems and clover indicate meadow foraging; woody bark indicates browsing on saplings or girdling behavior.
- Seed and fruit residues: presence of seeds reveals seasonal dietary items (berries, nuts).
- Inorganic inclusions: soil, grit, or sand can indicate burrow proximity or ground-level feeding.
- Seasonal variation: spring feces may show more green herbaceous material; late summer/autumn may include more seeds and tougher plant parts.
- Spatial patterning: scat piles near burrow entrances suggest latrine behavior; dispersed scat along feeding paths indicates active foraging zones.

Recommended field metadata: GPS, date/time, habitat description, proximate vegetation types, presence of burrow, and weather/season.

## Ethics / Safety

Safety and ethics are paramount.

- For handling:
  - Wear disposable nitrile gloves and eye protection when collecting or handling fecal samples.
  - Avoid direct contact; use sterile scoops/tongs and seal samples in labeled, leak-proof containers.
  - Do not inhale or create aerosols when sampling (no high-speed air drying or power tools).
  - Decontaminate surfaces and tools with 10% bleach or appropriate disinfectant after use.
- For analysis:
  - Send samples for parasite or pathogen testing only to certified labs with appropriate biosafety protocols.
  - Follow institutional biosafety and IACUC policies (if affiliated with a university/research institution).
- For field conduct:
  - Do not disturb animals, burrows, or habitat unnecessarily.
  - Obtain permission to sample on private or protected land; follow local wildlife regulations.
  - Practice Leave No Trace: minimize trampling and avoid creating persistent disturbance.
- For public engagement:
  - Emphasize that scat is biological material and can carry zoonotic agents; discourage sampling without training and PPE.
  - Provide clear consent and information to citizen scientists about what they may encounter and how to handle observations safely.

This project is educational; no invasive trapping or harm to animals is advocated here.

## Future work

Planned directions and open invitations:

- Standardize a public datasheet and digital form for citizen-science submissions (with required safety disclaimers).
- Expand photographic library across seasons and geographic ranges.
- Conduct controlled diet-to-scat correlation studies to refine plant-item identification from macroscopic remains.
- Integrate DNA metabarcoding and microbiome sequencing (partner with accredited labs) to quantify diet and gut health.
- Explore stable isotope analysis for longer-term dietary patterns.
- Develop a lightweight image-recognition classifier to assist field ID of scat vs. other small-mammal droppings.
- Publish a field guide / keys for distinguishing groundhog droppings from sympatric species.

Contributions welcome: open issues for protocol suggestions, data submissions, or collaboration proposals. Please follow contribution guidelines (see CONTRIBUTING.md) and respect safety/ethics notes above.
